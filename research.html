Research

Same Means, New End

A research-grounded argument that K-12 education should be reorganized around how students think, not prescribed content.

MA Thesis, George Washington University, Graduate School of Education and Human Development

For almost 250 years, curriculum theorists in the United States have circled around the same essential question: What is the purpose of schools? The question sounds simple, but it never has a simple answer. The country is too large, too diverse, and too divided in its beliefs and priorities to settle on one clear purpose.

Despite enormous social and technological change, the basic subject structure and age-graded sequences of schooling in 2025 would still be recognizable to students from 1925, and even 1825. When information is accessible instantly, and when students grow up surrounded by constant technological and cultural change, does it still make sense for the purpose of schooling to be centered on content?

My thesis argues that the purpose of school today should not be focused on what to learn -- it should be centered on how to learn. Students need the metacognitive skills to understand themselves, to evaluate information, to work across differences, and to adapt when the world and their reality shifts again. Content still matters, but it should not be the foundation of curriculum design. The foundation should be learning itself.

Core Framework

Key Concepts

Contextures

Learning Environments Rewoven

Interwoven learning environments that bring together themes, tasks, and sources so that students can work on how they learn, not just what they learn. A Contexture is not a 'unit on the American Revolution' -- it is a deliberate weave of experiences through which students are invited to notice, question, and reorganize their own thinking.

Cogniscence

Awareness Within Learning

Learning that includes awareness of how it is changing what you know and how you are able to know. When students cognesce inside a Contexture, they are not only acquiring information -- they are practicing learning-how-to-learn in ways they can carry into new situations.

The Six Metacognitive Habits

A Framework for Thinking About Thinking

Endospection (self-identity), Omniperegrination (wandering thought), Constellaration (pattern-finding), Refraction (critical analysis), Exospection (perspective-taking), and Synthesis (integration). Together, they define what it means to 'learn how to learn.'

The Axiometric Calculus

A Formal Notation for Meaning

A unified notation system for metacognitive operations grounded in the causal architecture of reality. The calculus describes itself -- it emerged through the processes it names. Paradoxes resolve through paradoxes. Each axiom holds because the others hold.

This work synthesizes insights from across curriculum theory and cognitive science. John Dewey's emphasis on experience as the basis of education. Jerome Bruner's spiral curriculum and the structure of knowledge. L. Dee Fink's taxonomy of significant learning. William Pinar's currere and the autobiographical method. Schema theory and how learners organize information. The result is not an extension of any one tradition but a new synthesis arguing for structural redesign.

The ability to learn how to learn matters more than any fixed body of knowledge.
