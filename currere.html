My Story

I have always been deeply passionate about history, and as an educator, I aim to help students appreciate the subject as more than just a collection of dates and events. History is a lens through which we understand societal trends, human motivations, and the interconnectedness of global developments.

My path has woven through classrooms, swimming pools, research offices, and graduate seminars - each experience shaping how I think about learning. I double-majored in History and Secondary Education at American University, where I learned about anti-racist pedagogy and the value of equity in education. I taught AP US History and AP Government at Bethesda-Chevy Chase High School, where I saw firsthand how curriculum structures shape what's possible for students. I co-founded Swim Safe with my brother - a community swim school that grew to serve over 500 families across seven years. And I'm currently finishing my MA in Curriculum Design and Pedagogy at George Washington University while working as a Project Director at a market research firm.

The thread through each experience is the same: I care deeply about how people learn, and I think the structures we build around learning matter more than the content we put inside them.

Teaching Philosophy

Core Values

Value

Know Your Students

It's important to make your students feel like people rather than just students. During my student teaching, I went to baseball games, swim meets, plays - every extracurricular my students participated in. This means knowing their home life and ensuring they have a safe learning and living environment.

Value

Representation Matters

It's extremely important to me that students feel seen and heard both by me and by their curriculum. They need to see themselves represented in order to resonate with the material. I want them to live it, understand it, feel connected to it.

Value

Adapt Always

We live in an ever-changing, fast-paced world. Whether it's learning to use new educational technologies thoughtfully, rethinking a lesson when students aren't grasping the material, or integrating tools like AI into your practice - flexibility is essential.

My long-term vision extends beyond the classroom. While I love teaching, I believe the greatest impact comes from redesigning the structures that shape learning itself. My thesis at GW argues that the purpose of schooling should center on metacognition - teaching students how to learn, not just what to learn. I'm interested in curriculum evaluation, education policy, textbook development, and any role where I can help build better systems for learning.

I am a historian by training, an educator by practice, and someone who has always worked across disciplines to figure out how things actually work before proposing how they should change.

How I Learn

Three Case Studies

From pre-K through eighth grade, I attended a small Armenian private school outside Philadelphia. I was one of the only non-Armenians - what they called an odar (օտար), meaning "other" or "outsider." There were seven kids in my graduating class. The curriculum was taught partly in Armenian, the holidays were different, the alphabet on the wall was different, and I was learning inside a system that was not designed for someone like me.

But here's what made it meaningful: I was embraced. The community brought me in. I learned Armenian, took part in the traditions, and by eighth grade I delivered my graduation speech in Armenian to the community I had become a part of - and received a standing ovation. People still mention it to this day. They joked that I should change my last name from Cosgrove to Cosgrovian so I could officially be Armenian. I was never going to be seen as fully Armenian - I was a blonde-haired kid with no Armenian heritage - but that was never held against me. It was what made the whole thing remarkable to them and to me.

This wasn't a story about exclusion. It was a story about what happens when a learning community actually does what it's supposed to do: welcomes someone in, holds them to the same standards, and lets them grow into something they weren't when they arrived. I crossed a cultural barrier and was accepted on the other side, not because the bar was lowered, but because the community made space.

Years later, when I started studying curriculum theory and culturally responsive pedagogy, I realized that my Armenian school years had given me something most curriculum designers never get firsthand: the lived experience of learning inside a structure that wasn't built around my identity, and seeing what it looks like when that structure still works. It changed how I think about who learning is designed for, whose assumptions get built into it, and what's actually possible when a community commits to inclusion rather than just talking about it.

When I was six, my mom signed me up for violin lessons at the Bryn Mawr Conservatory. I picked the violin because it was the only instrument I'd heard of. For the next four years, I hated it.

I practiced because my mom set the oven timer every day. I went to lessons because she drove me. My metrics of success were whether I could play a scale in tune - content-based standards set and assessed by someone else. I wasn't learning music. I was completing tasks. One afternoon, after four years of frustration, I snapped my bow in half. My mom took me to buy a new one the next day. With my own money.

Everything changed in seventh grade. I was the only student at my Armenian school who played an instrument, so when they needed someone to perform traditional Armenian music at Hantes - the school's end-of-year cultural celebration - I was the only option. The music was nothing like what I'd been trained on. Armenian modes don't map neatly onto western theory. The sheet music looked unfamiliar. For the first time, I couldn't rely on muscle memory or technique alone. I had to actually listen, to figure out why the music sounded the way it did.

That was the first time I wanted to practice. My mom didn't have to tell me. I was setting the oven timer on my own.

From there, music became a part of my life - three orchestras, two church bands, quartets with friends, AP Music Theory in high school, and an attempted music minor in college that I had to drop to finish my double major and Division I swimming in four years. But the real shift wasn't about accumulating experiences. It was about how I started learning. I taught myself the science of sound: wavelengths, air movement, analog vs. digital signal, timbre, why instruments sound different from each other, why a major third is not relationally tuned to the tonic. I fused music as an art and as a science, and that fusion expanded what it meant to know something.

Looking back, this is exactly the problem my thesis is trying to solve. For four years, I was learning violin the way most schools teach most subjects: here are the standards, here is the content, now perform. My success was measured by whether I could replicate what someone else defined as correct. I wasn't thinking about how I was learning or why it mattered. I was in what Bion would call a -K state - treating knowledge as something to endure rather than something to explore.

The shift to K - to actually wanting to understand - happened when the context changed. When I had to play Armenian music I didn't recognize, I couldn't just execute. I had to make sense of it. I had to build my own framework for understanding. That's metacognition. That's what Axiomatica is about. And it didn't come from a better teacher or a better textbook. It came from a reason to care.

I went from playing notes to learning music. That distinction - between performing content and understanding how you learn - is the distinction my entire body of work is built on.

What AI Is, and What It Is Not

AI is a tool. It is an extraordinarily powerful tool, but it is still a tool. It cannot feel, it cannot want, and it does not understand what it is saying. Large language models work by predicting the next combination of letters based on patterns in their training data. They are, at their core, a program of predictions. When people call AI "intelligent," they are projecting something human onto something that is not. I think that distinction matters a lot, especially in education, because if we misunderstand what the tool is, we will misuse it.

That said, AI is genuinely useful. It can analyze massive datasets that would take a person weeks to get through. It can support differentiated instruction, personalized feedback, and adaptive assessments. It can serve as a 24/7 tutor, a brainstorming partner, or a study tool for students who cannot afford one-on-one support. At the market research firm where I work, we used to manually code subgroups and connect themes across hundreds of verbatim files. Now an algorithm does the bulk of that work in minutes. The time savings are real. The efficiency is real. But none of that changes the fact that the person using the tool still has to know what they are looking for, and why.

This is where I think most conversations about AI go wrong. They focus on what AI can do without asking what it should be used for. Every major school district and educational organization in the country has released some kind of AI policy by now. Most of them say the same things: use AI responsibly, protect student data, don't let students plagiarize, train teachers on approved tools. Those are reasonable starting points. But almost none of them address the deeper question, which is whether AI should change what we teach, not just how we teach it. The policies are defensive. They manage the tool. They do not reimagine the curriculum.

I believe AI exposes a problem that was already there. If a student can generate a summary of the Bill of Rights in ten seconds, the issue is not that AI exists. The issue is that "summarize the Bill of Rights" was never a strong assignment to begin with. It tested content recall, and content recall was always the weakest foundation for learning. AI just made that visible faster. The better assignment would be: "Which rights are most vulnerable in today's climate, and why?" AI can still help a student research that question, but it cannot answer it for them, because the answer depends on who the student is, what they value, and how they think. That is the kind of learning AI cannot replace. And that is the kind of learning I think schools should be built around.

How I Learned It

I have never taken a computer science class. No bootcamp, no formal training in software development. My degrees are in History, Secondary Education, and Curriculum Design. But over the past year, I taught myself how to build full-scale applications from scratch, and I did it the same way I have always learned: I found a problem that mattered to me and worked backward from there.

At Cue Insights, our research team was spending hours on manual coding, trend analysis, and report formatting. I knew the process could be better, so I started learning. I built QGen, a full-stack data platform using Next.js, Python, Supabase, and Docker that automated our research pipeline and was adopted across the entire company. From there it compounded. I built AI-driven qualitative coding tools. I set up local AI agents for memory retrieval and workflow automation. I learned prompt engineering not as a technical skill but as a design discipline, because structuring a good prompt is honestly not that different from structuring a good lesson. You are guiding a process toward understanding, not just generating an output.

I also designed and published an instructional module called "Thinking Past the Prompt" that teaches educators how to integrate generative AI into their lesson planning. I built it on the R3 model I created: Real-Time, Real-World, Responsive. It was developed iteratively, tested by a diverse group of people from market researchers to swim coaches to high school teachers, and refined based on real feedback as I went. The whole thing was built with AI-assisted tools, which felt appropriate. If the module is about teaching people to use AI well, it should be built using AI well.

The point of all this is not that I am a developer. I am not. The point is that AI is learnable the same way anything else is learnable: you start with a reason to care, you figure out the structure of the thing, and you build from there. That is metacognition. That is exactly what my thesis is about. And it is the same process I went through with the violin, with Armenian, and now with code.

Where It Goes From Here

When calculators entered math classrooms, schools did not stop teaching algebra. They redesigned the curriculum so the calculator became a tool inside a harder set of problems. I think AI should work the same way for the rest of education. It is not a reason to lower expectations. It is a reason to raise them. If the tool can handle recall and summarization, then the curriculum should demand more than recall and summarization. It should demand interpretation, argumentation, reflection, and self-awareness. Those are the things AI cannot do, and those are the things that actually matter.

As a historian, I am trained to look at the present through the lens of the past. And the pattern is always the same. Every new technology follows a predictable arc: panic, prohibition, slow integration, and eventually, redesign. We saw it with the printing press, with television, with the internet, and with Wikipedia. AI is following the same arc right now. Most schools are somewhere between panic and slow integration. Very few have reached redesign. I think that is the opportunity.

What I want to see, and what I am working toward, is a version of schooling where AI is treated the way we should have treated every other tool: as something that makes the human side of learning more important, not less. If students can access any piece of content instantly, then the purpose of school cannot be content delivery. It has to be about teaching students how to think about that content, how to evaluate it, how to connect it to their own lives, and how to know when it is wrong. That is learning how to learn. That is what I believe schools should be for, and AI makes the case for it clearer than anything that came before.

I did not learn to code because I wanted to be a developer. I learned to code because I am a designer who ran out of tools. That instinct, to build what does not exist yet, is the same instinct that drives my research, my teaching, and my thesis.
