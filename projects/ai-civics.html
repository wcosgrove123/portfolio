AI + Civic Education

AI in Civics Curriculum

Reframing how civics teachers approach AI -- not by fighting it, but by teaching around it, through it, and past it.

Role: Curriculum Designer Context: GW -- EDUC 6402 and CPED 6608 Level: 12th Grade U.S. Government Focus: AI Literacy + Identity + Civic Participation
The Challenge

AI arrived before the curriculum did.

During student teaching at Bethesda-Chevy Chase High School, I watched it happen in real time: at least four papers had to be flagged as fully AI-generated in a single semester. And those were just the ones we caught. ChatGPT and tools like it had already become part of students' everyday workflows, whether we were ready or not.

Most schools responded in one of two ways: ban the tools entirely, or adopt them uncritically. Neither approach teaches students what they actually need. Trying to prevent students from using AI entirely seemed as pointless as banning calculators in math class. But unchecked reliance doesn't just risk factual errors -- it flattens nuance, reduces complex civic issues into surface-level summaries, and erodes the critical thinking skills that civics education exists to build.

The real fear isn't AI becoming sentient. It's people forgetting how to think.

The Approach

Reframe, don't fight.

When calculators became common in math classrooms, schools didn't give up on teaching algebra -- they pushed the curriculum harder. Students used calculators to design graphs, interpret matrices, and do pattern recognition. The instruments were harnessed as tools in the curriculum, not shortcuts.

I believe AI can, and should, work the same way for the humanities. If students can generate a decent summary of the Bill of Rights in 10 seconds, then the assignment can't just be "summarize the Bill of Rights." It needs to be: "Which rights are most vulnerable in today's climate, and why?" or "Which rights do you value most, and what does that reveal about your civic identity?"

This curriculum doesn't ban AI or blindly embrace it. It designs assignments that force revision, critique, and independent reasoning -- then deliberately integrates AI where it serves as a scaffold for deeper civic thinking, not a replacement for it.

Curriculum in Action

Two sides of one project.

This project spans two graduate courses and two complementary approaches: a policy analysis of AI in civics (EDUC 6402) and a full lesson plan grounded in identity and social movements (CPED 6608).

Thinking Past the Prompt

A policy-driven paper (EDUC 6402) analyzing student overreliance on AI in civics classrooms -- arguing that educators should reframe the way they teach around AI, not fight against it.

Identity and Social Movements

A 12th-grade U.S. Government lesson plan (CPED 6608) where students explore how identity shaped American social movements -- and reflect on their own civic identity before they become voters.

Intentional AI Use

Students research a cause they care about independently, then deliberately use ChatGPT to craft a letter to their representative -- combining critical thinking with practical AI application.

DCPS Standards Aligned

Curriculum maps directly to DC Public Schools Government standards -- evaluating marginalized groups' efforts for representation, analyzing social movements, and assessing civic participation over time.

Equity-Centered Design

The curriculum is designed so every student feels seen -- accounting for LGBTQ+ students, students of color, and diverse identities in how movements and civic participation are discussed.

Annotation Over Automation

When students use AI for brainstorming, they must annotate what they changed and justify why -- ensuring AI becomes a thinking partner, not a thinking replacement.

The Lesson

Fostering Independent Thinkers: To Know, To Question, To Act.

The CPED 6608 curriculum document -- titled "Fostering Independent Thinkers" -- is a fully developed lesson plan for 12th-grade U.S. Government. It asks students to investigate how personal identity has shaped American social movements throughout history, and then to reflect on what that means for their own civic participation.

The lesson fits into a unit on interest groups and social movements. Students work in small groups, each assigned a different movement. They research their movement's origins, identify the identity-driven motivations behind it, assess the perspectives of leaders, supporters, and opponents, and construct a collaborative visual analysis that demonstrates how identity, civic action, and democratic outcomes are connected.

The key insight is that civic action begins with personal identity -- what you value, what you believe, and what you feel is worth standing up for. Most of these students are about to become voters. The lesson gives them a framework to reflect on their own identity, challenge their assumptions, and understand how people throughout history have built movements and made change.

The theoretical framework draws on Waddell's developmental theory, Freire's critical pedagogy, and Mayo's work on inclusive education -- arguing that growth requires discomfort, that knowledge of oppression must lead to action, and that every student deserves to feel seen in the curriculum.

Connection to Research

Why this matters beyond the lesson.

This project connects directly to my thesis work in Axiomatica. The curriculum engages several of the metacognitive habits I've theorized: Refraction (examining AI outputs and social movements through multiple lenses), Exospection (understanding whose perspectives are included or excluded -- both in AI and in history), and Endospection (reflecting on one's own civic identity and what drives it).

The "Thinking Past the Prompt" paper makes the policy argument: AI is not going away, and neither is the responsibility educators have to prepare students for the world they're inheriting. If we ignore AI, students will find ways to use it in isolation. But if we integrate it deliberately -- framing it as a tool that complements, not replaces, the thinking process -- we can actually strengthen the core of what education is meant to do.

As Kumashiro argues, the most powerful ability in higher-level thinking is the ability to unlearn. It's up to teachers to reinvent curricula that account for these new developments. This project is my attempt to do exactly that.
